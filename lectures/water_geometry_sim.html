  • in optimization problems: cost surface

Types of surface water simulations

  • Surface water flow - overland flow accumulation
  • Flooding - spread of rising water level
  • Coupled flooding and surface flow - active research area

Both processes are controled by elevation surfaces

Flow over complex surfaces

  • Elevation surface represented by a bivariate function: $$z = f(x,y)$$

  • flow over this surface is driven by surface gradient $$ \nabla f = \left( {\partial f \over \partial x}, {\partial f \over \partial y} \right) = (f_x, f_y)$$
  • vector in the steepest slope direction (or aspect), and its magnitude represents tangent of slope angle.
  • slope is $$ \beta={\arctan}\sqrt{f_x^2+f_y^2} \qquad \beta[\%]=100 \sqrt{f_x^2+f_y^2} $$
  • aspect is $$ \alpha=\arccos {-f_y \over (f^2_x + f^2_y)^{1/2}} $$
  • where $f_x=\partial f / \partial x$ and $f_y=\partial f / \partial y$.

    note that the direction of flow is minus $\nabla f$, because gradient vector points upslope

    See GIS582 and supplemental material for methods to compute surface gradient, and its components slope and aspect (or partial derivatives)

    See Khan Academy for refresher on partial derivatives and gradient

Computing gradient vector

  • based on $\delta z_{max}$ in 3x3 window, discrete directions limited to 0,45,90, ...360 degrees, magnitude is rise/run
  • using partial derivatives of a suitable approximation function, such as polynomial or spline, (see GIS582 and GRASSbook - add link here)
  • using slope and aspect angle (this is variation of the above) $$ \partial f / \partial x = \tan \beta . \cos \alpha, \partial f / \partial y = \tan \beta . \sin \alpha$$

Stochastic methods

  • Independednt random variable $z$, is often distributed according to the normal distribution: $$ f(z | \mu,\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi} \sigma} e^{-(z-\mu)^2 / 2\sigma^2} $$

    where $\mu$ is the mean, $\sigma$ is the standard deviation, $\sigma^2$ is the variance, $e$ is exponential function, and $f(z | \mu,\sigma^2)$ is probability density function of the normal distribution

    Monte Carlo methods often require generating values that have normal distribution
